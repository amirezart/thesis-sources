## **Abstract** 

The number of university undergraduate courses in the area of interactive media is increasing. Many of these courses are based in the schools of art and design that have traditionally valued and focused on developing the aesthetic and artistic design skills of their students. However, because of the rapid changes in new technology the relation between the technology and design has become complex. This poses new challenges for the educators in this field. The main challenge is defining the role of programming in the curriculum and the relationship between ‘coding’ skills and ‘design’ skills. The article examines different conceptual models of programming and suggests that the concept of programming as artistic and creative practice and ‘programming as design’ would be more suitable for the art and design curriculum.

A quick search of UK higher education courses shows that, at the time of writing, there are more than 70 undergraduate courses related to interactive media/multimedia (UCAS web site http:// www.ucas.co.uk). These courses are based in various schools and departments, e.g., some are in schools of computer sciences, some in schools of media and communication and others in schools of art and design. Different disciplines have their own traditions. While computers and programming have by definition been an integral and important parts of computer science curriculum, the same cannot be said about media and communication or, in particular, art and design. 

Art and design is usually regarded as a creative discipline as opposed to a ‘scientific’ discipline such as computer science. Students who enrol on art and design courses do so with certain views as what will be expected from them and how their courses match their talents and capabilities. Art and design students predominantly see themselves as visually and creatively minded people rather than ‘scientifically’ minded. The same applies to the majority of teaching staff too. The main focus of art and design courses has always been on the development of students’ aesthetic and visual design knowledge and skills. As the impact of new technology has increased, art and design curricula have adapted to accommodate the use of digital technology. This can be seen in the use of digital still cameras and photomanipulation software in photography courses, digital video cameras and video-editing software in video courses, illustration and desktop publishing packages in graphic design courses, twodimensional and three-dimensional animation software in animation courses, sound and music generation and manipulation software in music courses and various computer-based design and manufacture packages in courses such as furniture and jewellery design and architecture. 

Although art and design has been receptive to the use of various software packages in its curricula there have also been some concerns about their potential negative impact (Marshall 2006). The main worry seems to be that ‘software skills’ are seen by students as design skills and not simply tools that are used to implement and 

produce the design, i.e., production tools. Teachers sometimes have to work hard to ensure that students concentrate more on the subject matter and not the tool, i.e., the software. While almost all art and design courses had to adapt to the increasing role and impact of computer technology, some courses owe their whole existence to new technology. Computer games, interactive media/multimedia and web design are among these. 

As the market demand for people with skills in the design and development of interactive multimedia artefacts increased, an increasing number of higher education institutions in the UK started to offer courses in this area. The area of creating interactive artefacts, or what Crawford (2003, 331) calls ‘interactivity design’, by nature ‘lies at the juncture between arts/humanities and science/ engineering’ or at the ‘intersections of information technology and creative practices’ (Mitchell 2003, 33). It is not surprising, therefore, that courses with similar sounding names are housed in different academic schools and departments which, understandably, are keen to keep their own culture. Many courses that are housed in the computer science departments keep their focus on software engineering and programming and hope that students would pick up any necessary artistic and visual design skills later in the workplace or that they would get a job as the developer in a team that includes professional designers. Most courses that are housed in the art and design schools usually concentrate on the artistic, creative and visual design skills and as much as possible try to stay away from programming. 

When it comes to programming, the questions that curriculum developers and teachers of interactive media in the schools of art and design face are many. For instance: should programming be taught at all? If so, how should it fit into the curriculum? However, before trying to answer these questions it is necessary to clarify what we mean by programming.

While there is agreement that programming involves the use of some kind of programming notation or language there is not always agreement as which notations are ‘true’ programming languages and which are simply ‘scripting’ languages. Historically, programming languages were general-purpose languages suitable for creating all kinds of applications and systems from scratch. Scripting languages on the other hand were always associated with, and embedded in, specific applications, e.g., a web browser, a word processor or an authoring tool. They allowed the end-users to enhance the capabilities of the application. Because they were designed for end-users rather than professional program- 

mers they were generally easier to learn and use (for a detailed explanation of the differences see Ousterhout 2001). 

But as a result of the rapid changes in programming tools and languages the once clear distinction between programming and scripting have started to blur. Many applications have standardised their ‘scripting’ languages so that they can more or less do what general-purpose programming languages do. As Bezroukov (2006) puts it: ‘scripting languages quickly evolve and each year they become more and more competitive with Java and C++ for developing mainstream enterprise applications’. A good example is the scripting language of Flash, i.e., ActionScript which is standardised as ECMAScript (read JavaScript) and turned into what is called ‘a general purpose embeddable language’. 

Blackwell (2002) puts forward a different view on what distinguishes programming from other activities. He points out that many people use the term ‘programming’ in relation to activities that are very different from the activities of professional programmers; for example, when people say that they are programming in HTML or they have programmed their VCR, or, perhaps more commonly these days, their satellite receiver, or their microwave or their boiler controls (Blackwell 2002, 208). He proposes that there are two main shared characteristics between all programming activities: ‘a) loss of the benefits of direct manipulation and b) introduction of notational elements to represent abstraction’ (p.208). So for Blackwell what is important in defining programming is the experience of the user and the cognitive effort that the user devotes to solving the problem. The intrinsic difficulty and complexity of the problem is not the main focus since ‘suitable software can make even complex problems seem simple’ (p.210). That is why, for example, typing a letter in a word processor is not seen as programming as the user benefits from direct manipulation, i.e., words appearing as the keys (the notation) are pressed, but writing Cascading Style Sheet (CSS) code to format that letter inside a web browser can be considered as programming if the user cannot see the changes as the code is written and has to load the page in the browser each time to see the effect

The fluidity of the concept of programming is particularly beneficial because it allows different disciplines to choose the definition that suits them best. In art and design, for instance, the concept of ‘idea generation’, ‘design specification’ and ‘design’ are already well established. So, perhaps the view of programming as writing the actual code is more suitable. In fact this is what many in art and design, and other disciplines, consider programming to be. On the other hand, following Blackwell’s criteria, we can confidently talk about programming regardless of whether the computer language we use to create our interactive artefacts is labelled as a markup language (HTML), design language (CSS), scripting language (Lingo, PHP), database language (MySQL) or programming language (ActionScript, JavaScript). In a sense this helps, in my view, to ‘democratise’ the programming field by removing the monopoly of one discipline. 

## **Should programming be taught?** 

Some interactive media courses may want to stay away from code and programming by using non-industry standard authoring environments that, although they may not be as powerful as the industry-standard ones, are easier to work with and do not require any programming. Others may use industry-standard environments that do not use any code, such as Adobe Flash Catalyst, or those who use code but limit the teaching to what can be done through the interface and perhaps with very simple programming commands with the hope that students will pick up the necessary programming skills themselves when they need to. 

However, it is no longer easy, nor wise, to exclude programming from interactive media courses. A quick look at the job adverts in new media shows that the market has a high demand for graduates with both creative and artistic design skills and practical development skills that 

include some level of programming. We also know that many students choose their courses mainly on the basis of their potential for future employment. Furthermore, the nature and the standards of interactivity of current digital artefacts, especially on the web, have changed and are becoming increasingly more intricate and sophisticated. What is regarded as interactive these days is different from what was accepted as interactive five years ago. The code-less approach to developing interactivity is no longer sufficient to meet the sophistication that people and the industry have come to expect from interactive digital artefacts. 

Furthermore, there is a growing acceptance that designing creative and innovative interactive artefacts requires programming and that the use of programming languages ‘are particularly important for specifying interactive behaviour and generative processes ... languages are critical to innovation’ (Mitchell 2003, 95). 

A good example is perhaps the three-dimensional online virtual world, ‘Second Life’ (http:// www.secondlife.com). In the last few years this environment has become very popular for creative experimentations. Many organisations, including universities, have developed their virtual presence there and many projects are trying to explore its potential for variety of purposes: artistic, commercial, publicity and educational. While users can create their presence and world from ready-made objects, to create richer and more complex interactivity one has to use Second Life’s scripting language ‘Linden Script’. The official Second Life site describes Linden Script as an ‘internal, eventdriven, C/Java-style language which allows you to control object and avatar behavior, up to and including mini-games and other complex programs’ (http://secondlife.com/whatis/scripting.php). In other words, there is a limit to the extent to which creative and interactive ideas can be materialised in Second Life, one of the largest programming environments in the world, without the knowledge of its programming language, Linden Script.

## **The impact on art and design curriculum** 

## **Bridge-building view of software construction** 

Despite the variation of what can be considered as programming and a programming language, when it comes to creation of software, including interactive artefacts, the dominant paradigm is based on software engineering. According to Blum (1996) this methodology is based on a fivestep classical problem solving: ‘What, How, Do it, Test, Use’ which is also the basis of scientific research ‘Hypothesis formulation, Experiment design, Experiment, Analysis, report or revise hypothesis’ and engineering disciplines such as bridge building ‘Requirements determination, Design, Build, Certify, and Use’ (p.251). This is clearly reflected in the classic ‘waterfall’ model of software life cycle with the following steps: Requirements, Design, Implementation, Verification, Maintenance (p.252, also see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterfall_model). There are various versions of the model which try to deal with what is perceived to be its weaknesses, e.g., the difficulty of completing one stage before moving to the next. However, the common feature of all the ‘engineering’ methodologies of software creation is the separation between ‘design’ and ‘implementation’ or ’construction’ (Fowler 2008, 4).

The scientific ‘bridge-building’ methodology of creating software has also had a great influence outside computer science and on the curriculum of many interactive media courses within art and design. This can be seen in the common separation between ‘design’ and ‘construction’ manifesting in the separation between ‘design’ modules and ‘programming’ modules or ‘workshops/surgeries’. Similarly, ‘design’ is predominantly regarded as the ‘creative’ and ‘intellectual’ activity worthy of the ‘academics’ to teach while ‘programming’ or ‘coding’ is seen as ‘technical’ and somehow ‘mechanical’ activity suitable for the ‘technicians’ to teach and do. ‘Programmers have nothing more to do with software creation than printers have to do with book creation’ (Crawford 2003, 334). 

A side effect of this separation is the confusion that is generated in real life when many who call themselves designers do the kind of things that ‘developers’ do and vice versa. It is not surprising therefore that when people try to match what they do on a daily basis with the terms ‘designer’ or ‘developer’ they find it difficult to fit their activities into only one of those terms and have to ask :’Are we designers or developers?’ (Johansson 2007). ## **Art and design students as ‘programming tourists’** 

There is a fundamental difference between teaching programming to computer science students and to art and design students. Computer science students are expected to become professional programmers who could be programming mission-critical applications in the future. Art and design students, on the other, hand are only end-user programmers who need programming to be able to create their often exploratory, experimental and artistic artefacts. They are not necessarily interested in the intricacies of the language for its own sake. Perhaps a good analogy would be the difference between teaching a foreign language to a linguist and to a tourist. The tourist needs the language to be able to communicate with people and explore the new environment and to get by. The linguist needs to understand the syntax, semantics and pragmatics of the language even if he or she never needs to communicate with a native speaker of that language. 

## **Alternative methodologies** 

Art and design needs alternative views of programming and constructing of interactive artefacts that are more in tune with its culture and traditions. Fortunately, the challenge to ‘bridgebuilding’ methodologies is gaining momentum and alternative views on programming, software construction, digital design and the role of code are being put forward. It is interesting to observe that as we move away from engineering paradigm we are moving closer to what can be seen as a more ‘artistic’ and ‘creative’ paradigm of software creation. 

## **Programming as artistic and creative practice** 

_The programmer, like the poet, works only slightly removed from pure thought-stuff. He builds his castles in the air, from air, creating by exertion of the imagination. Few media of creation are so flexible, so easy to polish and rework, so readily_ 

_capable of realizing grand conceptual structures._ (Brooks 1995, quoted in Hunt & Thomas 2001, 1) 

There has been an ongoing debate about whether programming is art or science. A lot of the debate is being carried out in the online discussion forums (e.g., see Kalish 2007; Littler 2005). One of the earliest arguments was put forward by Donald Knuth (1974) in his paper entitled ‘Computer Programming as an Art’. He reminds us that the Latin root of the word ‘art’, i.e., _ars_ , means ‘skill’ and that that the Greek word for art, i.e., _techne_ , is the root of both ‘technology and ‘technique’ (Knuth 1974, 668). He compares programming to composing poetry or music (p.670) and concludes that ‘programming is an art, because it applies accumulated knowledge to the world, because it requires skill and ingenuity, and especially because it produces objects of beauty’ (p.673). He does not deny, however, that programming can be seen as a science too, but he believes that these ‘two aspects nicely complement each other’. It is interesting to know that many programmers and computer scientists agree with Knuth and regard their work as a mixture of art and craft. 

Littler (2005) mentioned views of some famous computer scientists including some of the creators of popular programming languages, e.g., Bjarne Stroustrup, the creator of C++, Guido van Rossum, the creator of Python, who agree with Knuth’s views. Some have gone further and argue that in near future coding, as ‘fine art’, will be regarded as an ‘exciting new literary genre’ of its own (Barton 2005, 1). 

Hunt & Thomas (2001) in a paper called ‘The Art in Computer Programming’ see similarities between programming and other activities such writing and painting. 

In the year 2000 Richard Gabriel (Gabriel 2000) launched what he called ‘The Feyerabend Project: An Invitation to Redefine Computing’. The invitation raises many interesting issues regarding computing in general and programming in particular. He argues that in the first 50 years of computing, which he refers to as the ‘first era of computing’, we have ‘made a false start that can’t be fixed’ and requires a complete overhaul and a new start. He states that the first

206 era of computing started on wrong assumptions Faramarz Amiri about the nature of computing, what it would be used for and who would be doing programming, e.g., only computer scientists, mathematicians and engineers. But now, he goes on to say, a great deal of business and human activities are based on computing and digital technology. ‘Artists, craftspeople, writers, fishermen, farmers, tightrope walkers, bankers, children, carpenters, singers, dentists, and even some animals depend on computing, and most of … [these] people … want to have a say in how such software works, looks, and behaves’. He asks how we would feel if all computing was based on the needs of a particular group, say tightrope walkers. Perhaps his view is summarised in the last question of his invitation: ‘Computing is based on utility, performance, efficiency, and cleverness. Where are beauty, compassion, humanity, morality, the human spirit, and creativity?’. In an interview (Gabriel 2002) about ‘The Poetry of Programming’ he argues that producing software is a creative activity and writing code is ‘like writing poetry in that you write some of it, and then you respond to it, which triggers more creativity’ (p.2). For Gabriel, the software that is interesting to make is software that hasn’t been made before (p.1). He moves on to say that software developers should be also be trained in a similar way to students of Fine Art, using critical literature, critical context and a lot of practice (p.1). 

Graham (2003) puts forward a thought-provoking view in arguing that creative programming, which he deliberately calls ‘hacking’ to distinguish it from standard programming, should be viewed and practised more like ‘painting’ than engineering. He sees similarities between ‘hackers’ and other creative professionals such as painters, composers, architects, and writers because they are all ‘makers … trying to … make good things’ (p.1). He is against the engineering view that keeps design and implementation separate, and maintains that a hacker not only decides how to implement a specification but creates the specification through implementation (p.2). He maintains that programming should be done like painting and composing. So he chooses the term ‘sketching’ for his preferred 

style of programming to emphasise the process of ‘figuring out the program’ as it is being written (p.4). He recognises the malleability of digital design and sees a programming language as a means of ‘thinking of programs, not expressing programs you’ve already thought of’ (p.4). He expects the programming language to allow experimentation as opposed to adhering to strict rules. ‘We need a language that lets us scribble and smudge and smear, not a language where you have to sit with a teacup of types balanced on your knee and make polite conversation with a strict old aunt of a compiler’ (p.4). 

Blackwell (2005) looks at the programming language as a musical instrument in the context of live laptop music performances. He compares what conventional software engineers do with the musicians’ live programming on the stage. He, like Hunt & Thomas (2001, 5), picks on the fact that unlike software engineers, musicians, or artists in general, normally produce their work to commission rather than on the basis of a strict and pre-specified requirements. While the work should be consistent with the previous work and to some degree guided by the patron, there is a lot of room for ‘creative interpretation’ (Blackwell 2005, 206). That is why in live programming there is an integral link between the program and ‘the identity, personality and skill of the programmer’ (p.206). He proposes that live programming may be a better analogy for understanding how nonprofessional programmers and end-users program, and may serve as a model for future professional programmers too (p.207). 

According to Blum (1996) software engineering with its two-step process model, separating design from implementation, is based on ‘hardware engineering’ ignoring software’s unique properties (p.268). For dynamic applications and those with open requirements, he maintains, we should adopt ‘a sculptor metaphor’. We may start with sketching and analysis, but it is not that important that we stay absolutely faithful to the original plan. We continue sculpting till we are happy with the beauty of our artefact and when we have finished, the artefact’s ‘delivery constitutes its as-built specification’ (p.268). This is very different from the engineering view of having a

detailed specification first and then doing the implementation. For Blum, code is ‘the most detailed expression of the design’ (p.269). This view is shared by Reeves (1992, 1), who also maintains ‘that programming is not about building software; programming is about designing software’. That is why both Blum and Reeves maintain that the software manufacture, or building, is cheap, almost free, since once the design is done the building of this design is automatic and done by the computer when the program runs or is compiled. Blum’s sculptor metaphor is also in line with the concept of ‘software craftsmanship’ suggested by McBreen (2001). 

The desire for radical change in programming in order to make it more suitable for what, following Gabriel (2000), can be called the ‘second era of computing’, has led some (Noble & Biddle 2002) to propose a manifesto for ‘postmodern programming’ in the spirit of the ‘Bauhaus Manifesto’ of Walter Gropius which advocated the merging of the art and craft (Gropius 1919). In their article, Noble & Briddle rewrite the Bauhaus Manifesto and replace the word ‘building’ with the word ‘program’ and adjust the rest of the sentences to reflect this change while keeping the tone and spirit of the original manifesto. For example, where the Bauhaus Manifesto starts with ‘The ultimate aim of all creativity is building!’ they start their manifesto with ‘The ultimate goal of all computer science is the program’ (Noble & Biddle 2002, 1). They end their manifesto with the sentence: ‘Let us desire, conceive, and create the new program of the future together. It will combine design, user-interface and programming in a single form …’. Their proposed postmodern programming rejects one ‘overarching grand narrative’ (p.8) and instead includes many ‘little narratives’ and therefore allows for wide and diverse ways to program (p.9). 

I hope that the brief review above has demonstrated that there is desire and momentum to change the dominant engineering-based metaphors, methodologies and narratives in programming and software processes. This process has already started to have an impact on the real world and on the software industry as a whole. The best current example of an alternative view 

of programming and software processes that has found its way into the industry and even computer science and is rapidly gaining popularity is ‘Agile’ software development (Beck _et al._ 2001; Fowler 2008; Shore & Warden 2008) and the methodology of ‘Extreme Programming’ or XP for short (Baird 2002; Beck 1999, 2004; Beck & Fowler 2000; Fowler 2004, 2008; McBreen 2003; Noble _et al._ 2004; Shore & Warden 2008). Recently there have been some studies of using XP method in computer science programming courses with some success (Noble _et al_ . 2004). 

## **Teaching programming as design** 

There is not much research on the teaching methods, syllabus design and the impact of teaching programming as design to art and design students. However, Dorn & Guzdial (2006) examine the experience of a group of graphic designers who engage in ‘end-user programming’ and conclude that this group of designers could benefit from some aspects of formal teaching of programming and ‘software engineering’ (p.132). They also found that this group would readily reuse and share code, theirs or others, and that, for them, ‘programming serves as a means to save time and add features to their application’ (p.133). Their study suggests that the use of pre-existing samples, online documentations and learning from friends and co-workers were also common and perhaps can show what type of support can be made available to learners of this type. They conclude that teaching programming needs to be contextualised and meet the needs of the learners. ‘For end-users … learning to program is a natural progression. As a user has more and more sophisticated needs, she eventually outgrows the standard affordances of her tools’ (p.134). 

My own long experience of teaching programming to undergraduate interactive media students in art and design has shown me the students can and do learn programming skills to a high level. However, the programming has to be integrated into the process of design and development of interactive artefacts and not treated as a separate ‘techy’ programming module. Common problems are discussed and explained to the group as a whole before they return to individual or small group work to test out the solutions. This way, students can progress at their own pace while feeling supported by the tutor and the group. Programming in pairs (one of the features of agile programming model), code sharing and reuse (e.g., changing and reusing working code from previous projects) are very common among the students. The activities during the workshops are supplemented by the use of suitable textbooks and increasingly interactive software training DVDs or online training materials, especially those by www.lynda.com and www.adobe.tv, which the students have found useful during their self-directed learning outside the workshops. This approach has helped some of my students to become highly competent ‘programming tourists’. 

## **Conclusion** 

Programming is no longer an exclusive domain of a particular discipline. The time has come for art and design to embrace programming and make it its own. What is needed is a move away from the engineering model of software construction and towards adopting approaches that recognise the unique characteristics of digital design and the malleable nature of interactive artefacts. We 

need to find ways of using and embedding technology in our curricula so that it is more in harmony with art and design culture and its traditional creative practices. This would help us to operate more effectively in the ‘intersection of information technology and creative practices’ (Mitchell 2003, 33). 

The ‘craft’ tradition within art and design should make it easier for us to adopt and adapt non-engineering views of programming and agile methodologies and approaches. The idea of programming as a craft, as design and the idea of projects as ‘commissioned’ work allowing experimentation and creativity, all should help us to teach programming as an integral part of the ‘making’ of creative and interactive artefacts. Already people have suggested that programming needs to move out of ‘computer laboratories’ and into ‘computer studios’ (Woodcock & Bartlett 2005, 206). The idea of a ‘studio’ approach to programming is something that fits much better with the well-established culture of art and design where studios have always been a place for reflection, experimentation and creativity while making artefacts: 

_The place to fight design wars is in new markets, where no one has yet managed to establish any fortifications. That’s where you can win big by taking the bold approach to design, and having the same people both design and implement the product._ (Graham 2003, 5)
